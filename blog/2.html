Lessons from the Field: Research in Crisis Settings

5 minutes
July 2024

Conducting research in crisis settings—whether in conflict zones, disaster-stricken areas, or refugee camps—is vastly different from traditional fieldwork. Researchers face security risks, logistical barriers, and ethical dilemmas while striving to collect accurate and meaningful data. In such environments, adaptability is key, requiring innovative approaches to methodology and data collection.

Adapting Research Methods to Crisis Conditions

In volatile settings, conventional data collection methods—such as in-depth interviews or household surveys—can be impractical due to safety concerns or limited access to affected populations. Researchers must adopt alternative strategies, such as:

  • Remote data collection – SMS and phone surveys can help reach respondents when face-to-face interviews pose security risks.
  • Key informant interviews – Speaking with trusted local leaders or aid workers can provide insights when direct community access is restricted.
  • Satellite and geospatial analysis – Tracking displacement patterns or damage assessment via satellite imagery offers valuable context in inaccessible areas.

These methods ensure that essential data is still gathered without endangering respondents or researchers.

Cultural Sensitivity and Gaining Trust

In crisis research, building trust is just as important as data collection itself. Researchers must respect local norms, communicate clearly, and ensure participants understand how their information will be used. Engaging local communities from the start helps mitigate suspicion and encourages participation.

Additionally, ethical considerations are heightened in crisis settings. People who have experienced trauma may be hesitant to share personal details, making informed consent and confidentiality crucial. Researchers should prioritize "do no harm" principles, ensuring that participation does not put individuals at further risk.

Empowering Local Researchers

One of the most effective strategies in crisis research is training local field researchers. These individuals:

  • Have deep knowledge of the socio-political landscape.
  • Can communicate in native languages and dialects.
  • Are more trusted by affected communities than external researchers.

Building the capacity of local teams ensures that data collection remains resilient even when international researchers cannot be present.

Balancing Speed and Accuracy

In humanitarian crises, timely data is crucial for response planning. However, there is always a trade-off between speed and accuracy. Rapid assessments must be designed carefully to ensure that data remains reliable despite time constraints. Techniques such as triangulation—cross-checking information from multiple sources—help verify findings even in fast-paced research settings.

Ultimately, crisis research is about making informed decisions under pressure. By adapting methodologies, respecting cultural dynamics, and leveraging local expertise, researchers can ensure that their work contributes to more effective humanitarian interventions.
